                            Gabriel Rodrigues • 2 years ago

                            First of all let me tell you I love this series, the quality is amazing!

                            I can see how this would be a FANTASTIC fit in my game!

                            Think about Dwarf Fortress, if you're familiar with it: A bunch of little dwarves cramped in a room having fun, when suddenly a demon shows up!
                            Instead of them running around or being paralyzed due to being unable to path out of the room (since dwarves block each other) - and being picked off one by one - imagine them flooding the exits and being massacred - a much more convincing outcome!

                            I have a ton of questions, though:
                            How would you go about implementing this system in a grid-based game? I figure the pathfinding algorithm would need to have some cost related to attempting to walk out in a cramped doorway, so that in rooms where there was more than one exit a unit might prefer a doorway less cramped rather than the shortest path?
                            In the grid-scenario, how would units be able to walk out without overlapping, but still leaving no empty spaces (as in, crowding around the exit)? I guess I could use fluid spreading mechanics to attempt to simulate that...?
                            What would happen if there was another group trying to get INTO the room (say, a group of soldiers)? I guess when this happens in real life (say, firefighters attempting to go into a building on fire full of people trying to get out), the people trying to come in need to wait. So, to implement that, you'd have to have a "force" on the doorway indicating the direction of the movement, and a unit who wanted to get in would wait until the force was sufficiently low that it could be overcome?

                            Please keep the series running, it's awesome!

                                dovyski Gabriel Rodrigues • 2 years ago

                                Thank you very much! I'm really glad you like the series! About your game idea, it sounds awesome! I would love to play that.

                                About your questions, here are my thoughts:

                                How would you go about implementing this system in a grid-based game? What door should they choose?

                                I guess it works the same way in a grid-based system, since all steering behaviors are based on forces and points (or I got the wrong idea). I would implement the door choosing mechanism using a random function. It think it will produce very convincing results.

                                You can evolve that idea by testing the distance between the boid and the doorway (which is the point the boid is seeking) and how many boids there are around that point. You can weight them, so a boid will try to pick the best exit based on a simple environment check.

                                In the grid-scenario, how would units be able to walk out without overlapping, but still leaving no empty spaces (as in, crowding around the exit)? I guess I could use fluid spreading mechanics to attempt to simulate that...?

                                I guess I didn't get the grid idea. The last demo of the tutorial does that (fix the overlapping, etc), but if you are talking about a grid system like a chess board, that's a lot harder to fix. In that case, you must calculate a force to keep the boids on the grid, which is hard to predict and implement, since boids affect each other. Could you e-mail me at dovyski@gmail.com shedding some light on that?

                                What would happen if there was another group trying to get INTO the room (say, a group of soldiers)?

                                That would be nice to see! :D If you use different values for the ahead distance/radius for both groups, I think they will go through each other without overlapping.

                                Right now the queuing works because everybody is trying to go in the same direction. As the first in the line moves, the rest will follow. If a boid is going in the opposite way, it can stop the flow while waiting for someone to leave the way. Maybe nobody will move, because they are all waiting for each other.

                                You could fix that by adding a stronger separation force when both groups are close. E.g. "A firefighter is close, so the separation force is now 2X". It should make some room and "shake" things up, allowing everybody to pass.

                                Your idea of using a force indicating the direction of the flow is good, but it's a replication of the already existing forces. All boids trying to leave will create a force that pushes the boids trying to get in. That's why I think using a different separation force while they are close might work better.

                                P.S.: I found a serious bug: sometimes units go right through the wall:

                                About the bug you found, that's a nasty one :) It happens because some boids are under extremely strong forces produced by their neighbors. As a consequence, the collision avoidance force is not enough to keep the boid away from the wall, so it gets through the obstacle. It can be fixed with estimations, more forces and a better obstacle detection. I think that's a great idea for a future tutorial.

                                I hope you got some answer and can work on your ideas. I'm leaving for vacation with some pretty limited internet access. I will have to answer any other questions when I come back.
