                        dovyski HUSH-G 2 months ago

                        I am glad you like it! Your question is really interesting and the answer lays on the point of view. Both "desired_velocity" and "velocity" are a math vectors, which means they have a direction and a length/size/magnitude. In simple words, they can be seen as virtual "arrows" that point towards something.

                        If you think of forces (e.g. gravity, even velocity, etc), they can be represented by math vectors as well. They point to something. In the case of the velocity, it points towards the point where the entity is moving to. By using Euler integration, we add the velocity vector to the position vector, which makes the position change according to the size/length/magnitude of the velocity vector.

                        In the case of seek steering behavior, the "steering" force is nothing more than a math vector that is carefully calculated to push the entity towards the seek destination. "steering" is a force/vector, the same way "velocity" ou "desired_velocity" are forces/vectors. And that's the power in steering behaviors: since everything is a math vector, you can combine/add them easily. That's why you can make an entity seek ("seek steering force") a place at the same time it avoids an obstacle ("avoidance steering force"): you add both steering forces to produce a new one.

                        Is it clear now?

                                dovyski HUSH-G 2 months ago

                                You can call "steering" a velocity, but it is just a label. As you said, they are all vectors, so "steering" is a vector too. I called it a force because it makes more sense to me: it is something that will "push/pull" the entity towards a location.

                                When I say vectors can be combined/added, I mean math vector arithmetics. Say vectors V (velocity), S (seek) and A (avoidance), I can obtain a vector T by doing T = V + S + A. Vector T will be the sum vectors V, S and A and, in practice, it is a vector that can be used to move an entity by a determined velocity at the same time it tries to seek a location and avoid something.

                                Regarding your use of "a = F/m", I am not doing a full physics simulation here. "steering" is divided by mass just to make sure the steering force is proportional to the mass of the entity producing it. I am not using acceleration whatsoever in the tutorial :)

                                          dovyski Walextheone 2 years ago

                                          They are different, but can eventually be the same. "desired_velocity" is a vector always pointing to the target, it's a force that pushes the character straight to the target. On the other hand the "velocity" is a vector pointing to anywhere (which is the character's direction).

                                          The steering force is a vector calculated based on the desired_velocity and the character's current velocity. The steering force will be a vector that, added to the player's velocity, will make it turn until "velocity == desired_velocity". When it happens, the steering force will be 0 (as you said), meaning that no steering force is required anymore to push the characters towards the target, it's already on its way.

                                          Is it clear now?
